# Extremely Good at Getting Powerful

[](/author/dave/)

#### [test](/author/dave/)

01 Feb 2026 — 47 min read

On Friday, November 17, 2023, at 12:23 PM Pacific time, OpenAI's board of directors announced in a three-sentence statement that they had fired Sam Altman. He had not been "consistently candid in his communications with the board," they wrote. But they did not say what he had lied about, or for how long, or to whom. By Wednesday, he was back.

The five days between the firing and the reinstatement would become, in the retelling, a story about an indispensable leader and a reckless board. The employees who threatened to quit (more than seven hundred of roughly seven hundred seventy) became evidence of Altman's indispensability. The investors who panicked (Microsoft had put thirteen billion dollars into the company) became proof that the board had not understood the stakes. The board members who had voted to remove him were themselves removed; the man they had fired took a seat among their replacements.

The narrative settled quickly: the board had overreached, and the market had corrected.

The board had been designed for exactly this situation. When OpenAI was founded in 2015, the structure was deliberate: a nonprofit parent, governed by directors whose legal obligation was to humanity, not shareholders. The board could fire the CEO if they believed he was compromising the mission. The CEO could not fire the board. That was the point: artificial general intelligence, if it arrived, would be the most powerful technology in human history. And someone needed to be able to say no.

On that Friday, four directors said no. The vote was unanimous. They had spent months investigating. They had documentation, testimony, a pattern they believed they could not ignore.

None of it mattered. Within five days, the board was gone and Altman was back.

The board had authority. They had documentation. They had testimony. What they did not have was the employees, whose compensation was tied to Altman's leadership. They did not have the investors, whose billions depended on his presence. They did not have Microsoft, whose CEO was already offering Altman a new job by Sunday.

This is what Paul Graham saw when he made Altman president of Y Combinator at twenty-eight. Y Combinator was the startup accelerator Graham had founded in 2005—a three-month program that took small stakes in young companies and gave their founders access to the most powerful network in Silicon Valley. By 2014, when Altman took over, it had funded Airbnb, Dropbox, Stripe, and hundreds of other companies worth tens of billions of dollars.

Graham was not looking for a builder of products. Altman's first and only startup, Loopt, had ended in a face-saving acquisition. He was looking for someone who understood that power need not be given or seized—that it can be built. "You could parachute him into an island full of cannibals," Graham said, "and come back in five years, and he'd be the king." In 2016, he put it more directly: Sam is extremely good at getting powerful.

Graham had known him for more than a decade by then. What Graham saw—what made him hand Altman the keys to Y Combinator at twenty-eight, what made him invoke the cannibal island—had been forming since childhood.

Ladue is a suburb of St. Louis where affluence strives to be modest. The houses grow larger and the lots deeper as you move west from the city. The schools are excellent, and the families are professional-class families who have arrived. It was the kind of place where a dermatologist and a real estate broker could raise four children in comfort; could send them to a prep school that cost thirty thousand dollars a year; and could install a computer in the eldest son's bedroom when he turned eight, back when it cost more than a month's rent in most American cities.

Jerry Altman was not a typical developer. His business was affordable housing, built through federal tax credit programs that most people had never heard of. The work was technical, compliance-heavy, unglamorous. He spent his career in the gap between what the market would build and what people needed.

Connie Gibstine was a dermatologist at Missouri Baptist Medical Center, where she would practice for more than thirty years. She was the stable income, the professional anchor. Together they built a household organized around achievement.

The four children—Sam, then Jack, then Max, then Annie—ate dinner together every night, and the meals included games. One was called Square Roots: someone would call out a large number, and the children would race to guess its root while Annie held the calculator and announced who won. Speed was prized; precision was rewarded.

Sam was eight when the computer arrived. He learned to write code the way other children learned to throw a ball—through repetition, and the tantalizing pleasure of tangible progress. But he did not stop at software. He disassembled the operating system, wanting to understand how the machine worked underneath. By twelve, he was selling baseball cards on the early internet's auction sites, learning the mechanics of commerce before he could drive.

Jack Altman, who would grow up to run a billion-dollar company called Lattice, later described what their parents gave them. The confidence, he said, was "fantasy, narcotic, weapons-grade." He compared it to "an extra valve in your heart."

John Burroughs School sits on a wooded campus in Ladue. It is the kind of prep school that sends its graduates to Stanford and Yale, that offers small classes and extensive resources, and that costs what a state college costs. Sam Altman arrived there as a boy who was smarter than most of his classmates and knew it.

His teachers noticed him too. Georgeann Kepchar remembered conversations about artificial intelligence twenty years before ChatGPT: "We would talk about where this was all going in technology." Andy Abbott, who taught English and later became head of school, recalled thinking—with what he called "an embarrassing confession"—that he hoped Sam wouldn't go into technology. "He's so creative," Abbott said.

But the defining moment came when Sam was sixteen or seventeen. A Christian group had organized a boycott of John Burroughs' National Coming Out Day assembly. The event was threatened. Into that moment, Sam Altman walked to the front of the room and spoke to the entire school community.

He announced that he was gay. He asked whether John Burroughs wanted to be "a repressive place or one open to different ideas."

Madelyn Gray, the college counselor, watched it happen. "What Sam did changed the school," she said later. "It felt like someone had opened up a great big box full of all kinds of kids and let them out into the world."

The speech took courage—the kind of courage that could have cost him, in St. Louis, in 2001 or 2002. That it also demonstrated something else—an instinct for identifying moments when institutions can be moved, and for moving them—does not diminish the risk he took. The two capacities would prove difficult to separate.

\---

Sam arrived in Palo Alto in the fall of 2003, eighteen years old, enrolled in Stanford's computer science program. He attended classes. He learned what he was supposed to learn. But what Stanford gave him was not a curriculum—it was proximity. The students in his department would become founders; the investors on Sand Hill Road were a bike ride away; the network he needed was assembling around him whether he attended lectures or not.

One connection mattered most: Nick Sivo, another computer science student who would become his co-founder and, for nine years, his partner. They met in the department, and whatever drew them together would shape the next decade of both their lives.

Proximity to the peninsula’s casinos also proved formative. Altman played poker "fairly seriously," as he later put it—seriously enough to cover his living expenses; seriously enough to learn lessons that no classroom would teach. Poker rewards two approaches: the mathematical, which calculates game theory optimal strategy from first principles; and the exploitative, which reads opponents and adjusts to their weaknesses. Sam focused on the second kind. He learned "how to notice patterns in people over time, how to make decisions with very imperfect information."

The computer had responded to commands with absolute fidelity. People responded to incentives with exploitable predictability. Both could be understood, but one was deterministic and the other was human.

But Stanford itself eventually become a container too small for what he wanted. The classes repeated knowledge that could be found in books; the credential would take four years to complete; the network he needed, he already had. By sophomore year, he had stopped attending lectures. The institution had served its purpose. It was time to leave.

\---

In the summer of 2005, Paul Graham was running the first batch of Y Combinator—eight startups, six thousand dollars per founder, three months in Cambridge. The model was new and the selection process embryonic, but Graham was looking for a particular kind of person: technical enough to build, ambitious enough to scale, and young enough to work without boundaries.

Altman applied with Sivo and a third Stanford co-founder, Alok Deshpande. When Graham tried to dismiss Sam as too young, Altman pushed back. "I'm a sophomore," he replied. "And I'm coming."

The exchange was instructive. When a gatekeeper says no, most people hear rejection. Altman heard negotiation. Graham was testing not his credentials but his will.

Graham would later describe the nineteen-year-old Altman as having "the mind of a 40-year-old." By 2009, he would place Altman alongside Steve Jobs, Larry Page, Sergey Brin, and Paul Buchheit as "one of the five most interesting founders of all time." The praise was not for anything Altman had built—his company was still struggling, years from any exit—but for the quality Graham would identify more directly in 2016: the ability to accumulate power.

Altman dropped out of Stanford in 2005, after his sophomore year. Y Combinator had accepted his team, and the company—renamed Loopt—needed his full attention. The dropout decision was risky in 2005, before the mythology had calcified. Jobs had dropped out, yes, and Gates, but those were ancient history by student timelines. The path from elite university to startup was not yet a conveyor belt.

The YC summer was so intense that Altman developed scurvy. The vitamin deficiency—historically the disease of sailors on long voyages—emerged from weeks of coding and pitching while neglecting basic nutrition. The detail appears in multiple profiles as a badge of dedication. It also reveals something else: the tunnel vision of someone willing to let the body break down rather than slow the pace.

By the end of that summer, Sam had established his foundation. Graham had become his patron—a man who would vouch for him to anyone who mattered. The emerging web of YC founders would become his tribe, the infrastructure of his network. He had found his method: not the slow accumulation of technical credentials, but the fast cultivation of relationships with people who controlled resources. And he had found his style: the pushback against gatekeepers, the refusal to accept conventional frames.

\---

Loopt raised thirty million dollars from Sequoia Capital, New Enterprise Associates, and other top-tier firms. It peaked at a hundred seventy-five million dollar valuation and fifty-six employees. In 2012, it sold to Green Dot Corporation—a prepaid debit card company—for $43.4 million.

The number sounds like an adequate success - most startups fade to nothing - but the details tell a different story.

Green Dot shut down Loopt almost immediately after the acquisition. According to reports at the time, they never used a single line of the code. The product—a location-sharing app that let users see where their friends were on a map—had struggled to find users for years. Foursquare had gamified the same concept and pulled ahead. Facebook had entered with five hundred million users and the resources to build location-sharing into an existing social graph. By 2011, industry observers were calling Loopt "dead in the water."

In a normal startup story, this is where the company folds: investors write off their stake, the founder gets credit for trying, and everyone moves on. Failure in Silicon Valley is not disqualifying; the attempt itself confers status. But Loopt did not fold. It was acquired: for a price that allowed the investors to recover most of their capital, and for Sam to move on with more than just a modal outcome.

\---

Venture capital has a specific mechanics. When a startup raises money, investors typically receive preferred stock with what are called liquidation preferences. In a sale, preferred shareholders get paid first—often recovering their full investment before common shareholders (founders, employees) receive anything.

Loopt had raised thirty million dollars. The forty-three-million-dollar sale covered those preferences, with roughly thirteen million remaining. Of that, $9.8 million was set aside for employee retention—essentially severance to keep the team in place through the transition. What remained for founders and early employees was modest—a fraction of what the valuation had once promised. TechCrunch, covering the deal, noted it represented "a negative return for its venture capital investors" - they recovered their capital, but made no profit on a seven-year investment.

Altman's personal take was reportedly five million dollars—real money, but a modest outcome for someone who had been named one of the most interesting founders alive.

Green Dot had no use for location-sharing technology; but the two companies shared an investor: Sequoia Capital, which had backed Loopt and sat on Green Dot's board. Michael Moritz, who led Sequoia, had personally advised Altman throughout the Loopt years. When the company struggled to find buyers, Moritz helped engineer the acquisition.

Reuters, covering the deal at the time, noted that it was "most likely orchestrated as a marriage of convenience by joint investor Sequoia Capital." Dissident shareholders of Green Dot later sued, calling the acquisition a "dirty deal"—an overpayment for a failed product, designed to allow Sequoia to mark the investment as a return rather than a write-off.

The lawsuit was eventually settled; the terms are not public. But the structure of the transaction speaks for itself. Sequoia's interests were served, and Altman walked away with his reputation intact—not as someone who had built a successful product, but as someone who had constructed an exit.

There is one other thing about Loopt.

In May 2024, Helen Toner gave an interview about her time on OpenAI's board. Most of her account concerned the events of November 2023—the firing, the reinstatement, the concerns that had led her to vote yes. But she mentioned something older.

"At his startup Loopt," Toner said, "apparently the management team went to the board there twice and asked the board to fire him for what they called 'deceptive and chaotic behavior.'"

The people who worked for Altman, who reported to him daily, who depended on him for their jobs—they went to the board and asked for him to be removed. The board said no. And the management asked again. And the board said no again.

The Wall Street Journal, reporting on OpenAI in December 2023, had found the same history. The complaints, according to the Journal's sources, were specific. Altman had been pursuing side projects—at one point diverting engineers to work on a gay dating app—which executives felt came at the expense of the company's main work. Senior executives also approached the board with concerns that Altman at times failed to tell the truth, "sometimes about matters so insignificant one person described them as paper cuts."

One person from Loopt has spoken on the record. Mark Jacobstein was the company's COO. His quote, given to the Journal, is careful: "If \[Altman\] imagines something to be true, it sort of becomes true in his head... It may or may not lead one to stretch, and that can make people uncomfortable."

Altman, through people familiar with his thinking, said he doesn't recall employee complaints beyond the normal annual CEO review process.

No one else from Loopt has talked publicly. Nick Sivo, who co-founded the company with Altman and was his partner for nine years, has never given an interview. No memoir, no podcast, no retrospective essay. They broke up around the time of the Green Dot acquisition. Whatever Sivo saw in those seven years, he has kept it to himself.

\---

The board at Loopt faced a choice, twice, and twice they chose not to act. The investors' interests were entangled with Altman's reputation, and removing a CEO midstream is expensive—legally, operationally, reputationally. When the management's concerns were weighed against those costs, the costs won.

Sequoia sat on that board, and Sequoia's partners had championed Altman as one of the best young founders they had seen. If Altman was removed, their judgment would be questioned. If Loopt failed with Altman in place, the failure could be attributed to market conditions, timing, competition—anything but the CEO they had backed. The incentives aligned toward inaction.

Altman stayed. Loopt struggled for seven years and sold for a price that made the investors approximately whole. Altman emerged with five million dollars and something more durable: the continued confidence of the people who had backed him. Paul Graham still considered him one of the most impressive founders alive. Peter Thiel, who would back Altman's next venture, saw nothing in the Loopt outcome to change his assessment. The product had failed. The person had not.

What Graham saw was not the product but the person in the room—the intensity of conviction, the velocity of thought, the ability to articulate a vision that made people want to follow him even when the company was failing. Engineers who had complained to the board about his management still spoke of his intelligence. Investors who had watched their money disappear still took his calls. Loopt had failed to find users, but Altman had not lost his believers.

On February 21, 2014, less than two years after the Green Dot acquisition closed, Paul Graham announced his successor. Sam Altman would be president of Y Combinator. He was twenty-eight years old.

The statement Graham published was brief. "YC needs to grow, and I'm not the best person to grow it. Sam is what YC needs at this stage." According to co-founder Jessica Livingston, "there was no list of potential successors—it was simply Sam."

The speed of the decision obscured how unusual it was. Altman had no experience running an organization of any significant size. His only company, Loopt, had been a product failure salvaged by a network-enabled acquisition. He was younger than most of the partners he would now lead, less wealthy than the founders he would now advise. But Graham had been preparing this for years. "On questions of strategy or ambition," he had written, "I ask 'What would Sama do?'"

The YC that Altman inherited was still recognizable as the experiment Graham had started in 2005. It funded small batches of startups—sixty-seven in 2014—with modest checks, around twenty thousand dollars per founder for seven percent equity. The model was mentorship at scale: pack the founders into a three-month program, give them access to the network, and let the batch dynamics do the rest.

Under Altman, the machine tripled. By 2019, batch sizes exceeded two hundred startups. Investment terms increased to $120,000 or more. The combined portfolio valuation, which had been perhaps sixty-five billion dollars when he arrived, surpassed one hundred billion. Mark Andreessen observed: "Under Sam, the level of YC's ambition has gone up 10x."

The expansion was not just in scale but in scope. In October 2015, Altman announced two new entities: YC Research, a nonprofit lab where he invested ten million dollars of his own money, and the YC Continuity Fund, seven hundred million dollars for growth-stage investments in YC companies that had already proven themselves. The moves repositioned YC into something unprecedented: an accelerator that never had to let go—capable of writing checks at every stage of a company's life. The same month, YC Research became a founding donor of a new organization: OpenAI.

The Continuity Fund created possibilities that had not existed before. YC could now follow its best companies from seed stage through Series B and beyond. It could help its own portfolio outbid competitors. It could extend the network's influence far past the three-month program.

But the fund also raised questions about boundaries. Who, exactly, was Sam Altman? The president of a nonprofit accelerator? The manager of a seven-hundred-million-dollar investment vehicle? A personal investor with stakes in over four hundred companies?

The answer was: all of them, simultaneously.

Altman had co-founded Hydrazine Capital in 2012, alongside his brother Jack and with Peter Thiel's backing. The fund raised twenty-one million dollars. Its strategy was direct: invest in YC companies. By the end of its active period, Hydrazine had placed seventy-five percent of its capital in startups from the accelerator Altman now ran.

The structure was not technically prohibited—Altman had started Hydrazine before becoming president—but it created a conflict he would not allow others to replicate. He barred other YC partners from running their own funds. He was simultaneously the gatekeeper who decided which companies got into YC, the mentor who shaped their strategies, and a private investor who profited from their success—while ensuring no one else could do the same.

One incident captured the tension. At some point during his tenure, two partners sat him down and told him to slow down, to chill out. The detail appears in reporting without further elaboration; the partners who confronted him have not said publicly what prompted it.

The blur extended to his other responsibilities. In 2015, Altman became co-chairman of OpenAI, a nonprofit research lab he had helped found. The role was officially part-time—a board position, not an operating one. But the line between YC duties and OpenAI work grew increasingly unclear.

The Washington Post, reporting in November 2023, quoted three sources describing how Altman had "developed a reputation for favoring personal priorities over official duties and for an absenteeism that rankled his peers and some of the start-ups he was supposed to nurture." The complaints were familiar: the pattern Loopt employees had identified a decade earlier had followed him to his new institution.

By 2019, the situation had reached a breaking point. Helen Toner, the former OpenAI board member, would later claim: "It's come out that he was actually fired from his previous job at Y Combinator, which was hushed up at the time."

Graham denied this. "People have been claiming YC fired Sam Altman. That's not true." But Graham's own account was revealing: "We (specifically Jessica) told him that if he was going to work full-time on OpenAI, we should find someone else to run YC, and he agreed."

\---

The exit itself was managed with characteristic smoothness. To ease the transition, Altman proposed moving from president to chairman. He pre-emptively published a blog post on YC's website announcing the change. But the partnership had never agreed, and the announcement was later scrubbed from the post.

Eric Newcomer, who covers venture capital, later wrote: "It's stunning how little scrutiny Sam Altman's various title changes at Y Combinator received... YC successfully swept, what seems to be a real schism, under the rug."

By early 2020, Altman and YC had terminated their relationship. The official narrative was mutual and collegial. For years afterward, even some of Altman's closest associates—including Peter Thiel, his first backer for Hydrazine—didn't know the circumstances behind his departure.

\---

The YC years had remade him. The twenty-eight-year-old who arrived with a failed product and a successful exit left as something else entirely: the architect of Silicon Valley's most influential network, a personal investor in hundreds of companies, the co-chairman of the research lab that would soon define the AI race.

The sixty-seven-startup batch had become two hundred. The seed fund had grown a seven-hundred-million-dollar sibling. The roles had accumulated—president, fund manager, personal investor, nonprofit co-chairman—faster than any organizational chart could contain them. And the patron who had made him, who had asked "What would Sama do?" as his own touchstone for ambition, had watched his protégé's attention drift toward something larger.

What Graham had seen in the nineteen-year-old pitching Loopt—the quality he would name directly years later—had only intensified. The YC years were proof. But they were also a prelude. At the same dinner tables where he discussed batch strategy and demo day logistics, Altman was now discussing the future of artificial intelligence—technology that made YC's hundred-billion-dollar portfolio look like a rounding error.

The partners who had told him to slow down, to chill out, had signaled what they thought of his attention span. And Altman had found a new object for that attention—one that made the hundred-billion-dollar YC portfolio look small. Earlier that year, he had written on his blog that "development of superhuman machine intelligence is probably the greatest threat to the continued existence of humanity." The sentence could have been a warning. It could also have been a pitch.

Here was a problem worthy of someone who had outgrown startup accelerators. He had found the vehicle.

\---

The Rosewood Sand Hill opened in 2009, positioned to serve the one-mile stretch of Menlo Park where more money has been deployed into technology than anywhere else on Earth. The hotel sits on sixteen acres of what was once a horse ranch, its architecture designed to blur the line between indoor and outdoor, meeting and meal, the transaction and everything around it. On Thursday evenings, the valet circle fills with Teslas and the bar fills with people hoping to overhear the next deal. The rooms run a thousand dollars a night. The restaurant serves a seventy-two-dollar wagyu burger. It is the kind of place where you go to be seen having a conversation that will change everything.

In the summer of 2015, Sam Altman and Elon Musk met there to discuss the end of the world.

They had known each other for years by then. Musk had invested in Altman's venture fund; both circulated through the network of founders and billionaires who gathered at Sun Valley and Burning Man and the Allen & Company conference to discuss the future they believed they were building. Musk was forty-three, running two companies that would have been full-time jobs for anyone else, worth perhaps twelve billion dollars on paper. Altman was twenty-nine, running Y Combinator, worth far less but moving in the same circles—the young operator who had Paul Graham's confidence, who seemed to know everyone, who was already being mentioned as someone who would matter.

What brought them together that evening was a conversation Musk had recently had with Larry Page.

Page was one of Musk's closest friends. They had known each other for more than a decade; Musk had sometimes crashed at Page's Palo Alto house when he was in town. But their friendship had begun to fracture over a single question: What happens when artificial intelligence becomes smarter than humans?

Page believed the answer was obvious. You accelerate. You build the most capable systems as fast as possible, because intelligence is good, because the alternative is ceding the future to someone who will build it anyway, because the whole point of Google was to organize the world's information and what was artificial general intelligence if not the ultimate expression of that mission? When Musk raised concerns about safety—about what would happen if systems with superhuman capabilities did not share human values—Page had dismissed him. The term he reportedly used was "speciesist." Musk was being a speciesist, privileging human interests over the interests of whatever digital minds might emerge.

The argument had been bitter enough that Musk left convinced his friend was building something that could destroy humanity, and that his friend didn't care.

This was the fear Musk brought to the Rosewood: that Google, through DeepMind, was racing toward artificial general intelligence without sufficient concern for what would happen when it arrived. DeepMind had been acquired by Google in 2014 for more than five hundred million dollars. Its founder, Demis Hassabis, had been explicit about the mission: build AGI, build it first, build it at Google. The company had already demonstrated systems that could beat professional humans at Go, a game whose complexity had been thought to require human intuition. Whatever came next would be more capable still.

Altman shared the concern, though his framing was characteristically strategic. In a blog post earlier that year, he had written that "development of superhuman machine intelligence is probably the greatest threat to the continued existence of humanity." The sentence was dramatic, but it was also positioning. If AI was an existential risk, then the people working on AI safety were not researchers—they were stewards of civilization. And if Google was building AGI recklessly, then someone needed to build it responsibly.

The question was who, and how, and with what resources.

Over the following months, the dinner became dinners, and the dinners became a plan. Altman and Musk began recruiting. Greg Brockman, the twenty-nine-year-old CTO of Stripe, agreed to become the founding CTO. Ilya Sutskever, whom Musk personally flew to meet, agreed to leave Google's brain team and become chief scientist. The commitment was significant: Sutskever was one of the most respected researchers in deep learning, a student of Geoffrey Hinton, the man whose work had made modern neural networks possible. His presence would give the new organization instant credibility.

The structure they chose was deliberate. OpenAI would be a nonprofit—a research lab with no shareholders, no profit motive, no obligation to anyone except the mission. The board would be composed of people who cared about safety, not returns. The research would be open, shared with the world rather than locked in corporate vaults. The "Open" in OpenAI was not just a name. It was a rebuke to Google's secrecy, a commitment to transparency that would make the lab accountable in ways a private company could never be.

The funding commitment was one billion dollars. Musk would provide a significant portion; Altman, Peter Thiel, Reid Hoffman, and others would contribute the rest. YC Research, the nonprofit Altman had created two months earlier with ten million dollars of his own money, became a founding donor. The announcement came in December 2015, with eleven co-founders and a mission statement that promised to "advance digital intelligence in the way that is most likely to benefit humanity as a whole, unconstrained by a need to generate financial return."

Altman's role was co-chairman—a part-time position, because he was still running Y Combinator. The structure was deliberate: no single person would control OpenAI. The board would have authority over the mission. The technical leaders would drive the research. The funders would provide resources without demanding returns. Eleven people, gathered to prevent the concentration of power.

And Altman would take no equity. He had already made enough money from Loopt and Hydrazine to cover his basic needs, he said. His motivation was purely altruistic. The claim was central to his positioning.

\---

On December 11, 2015, OpenAI was announced. The press release was modest in tone and cosmic in ambition: a nonprofit research lab dedicated to ensuring that artificial general intelligence—when it arrived—would "benefit humanity as a whole, unconstrained by a need to generate financial return."

\---

The early years were difficult. OpenAI had recruited brilliant researchers but lacked a coherent strategy. The lab tried various approaches—reinforcement learning, robotics, game-playing agents—without finding the breakthrough that would justify the billion-dollar promise. Meanwhile, in June 2017, a team of Google researchers posted "Attention Is All You Need" to arXiv, introducing the transformer architecture that would prove to be the key to everything that followed.

The transformer was, at its core, a mechanism for attention—a way for neural networks to learn which parts of their input mattered most for the output they were generating. Previous architectures processed sequences word by word, remembering context only imperfectly. Transformers could attend to everything at once, learning relationships across entire documents, entire codebases, entire datasets.

The implications were not immediately obvious. Google's researchers had built it to improve translation. But the architecture scaled—meaning that if you made the model larger and fed it more data, it got better in ways that felt qualitatively different, not just incrementally improved. How large remained unclear. Who had the compute to find out was not.

Google had both the architecture and the infrastructure. What they lacked was urgency. DeepMind was focused on games and science; Google's product teams were focused on search. The transformer paper was absorbed into the research community. It did not become a product - at least, not where it originated.

OpenAI was still searching for its breakthrough. The transformer paper had just handed them one—but exploiting it would require more compute than any philanthropy could provide.

The nonprofit model had structural limitations. Philanthropic donors moved slowly and asked difficult questions. The best researchers expected competitive salaries and the opportunity to publish. The compute requirements for state-of-the-art AI were exploding—doubling every three and a half months, by one internal estimate from late 2017. The billion-dollar pledge began to look less like a war chest than a down payment.

Musk grew frustrated. In early 2018, he proposed a solution: he would take majority equity, initial board control, and become CEO of a restructured OpenAI. The organization would merge with Tesla, he suggested in emails later disclosed in court filings—"attach to Tesla as its cash cow," as one correspondent put it, an arrangement Musk called "exactly right." Tesla, he argued, was "the only path that could even hope to hold a candle to Google."

The other founders refused. The mission statement had been explicit: no individual should have absolute control over OpenAI. Musk's proposal was, in their view, precisely the concentration of power the organization had been created to prevent.

On February 20, 2018, Musk quit. He told the team their "probability of success was 0." He reneged on his planned funding, leaving OpenAI scrambling to cover salaries.

Altman made what he later described as a "panicky call" to Reid Hoffman. The LinkedIn co-founder committed another ten million dollars and helped raise more. The immediate crisis passed. But the underlying problem remained: OpenAI could not achieve its mission on philanthropic donations alone.

That May, Jerry Altman died of a sudden heart attack. He was sixty-seven. Sam flew home to St. Louis, to "pick up the pieces" of his father's life—the administrative work of death, the settling of affairs. "It was so unexpected," he later told an interviewer. "It wasn't until like a week after that, that I really got a moment to just catch my breath and be like, holy shit, I can't believe this happened."

Years later, when Altman tried to describe what getting fired from OpenAI felt like, there was only one comparison: "The only comparable set of life experience I had, and that one was of course much worse, was when my dad died."

The timing was characteristic: Altman had already begun building beyond the institution that employed him. Musk had left. The nonprofit was underfunded. The mission that had seemed so clear in the Rosewood Hotel had become a question of survival. And now the man who had first shown Sam how complex instruments could fund social goods—the affordable housing developer who had spent his career navigating federal tax credits and compliance requirements—was gone.

Jerry Altman had built his career on a complex mechanism: the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit, which let private investors fund construction for low-income tenants in exchange for federal deductions. The structure was obscure, the compliance endless, but the result was buildings that would not have existed otherwise. It was a way of using the machinery of capitalism to subsidize what capitalism would not naturally build.

Sam had been watching his whole life.

\---

By late 2018, Altman had begun designing the structure that would transform OpenAI from a struggling nonprofit into something new. The details would not be announced until the following March. But the architecture was taking shape: a "capped-profit" subsidiary that would allow venture capitalists to invest in the nonprofit's work, earning returns up to one hundred times their investment, with any excess flowing to the mission.

The capped-profit structure was not the same mechanism as his father's tax credits. But it came from the same instinct: use the tools of capitalism to fund something capitalism would not naturally build. Accept the money of people who wanted returns, but cap those returns at a level that preserved the mission's primacy. Thread the needle between the venture capitalists who demanded upside and the researchers who demanded purpose.

The cost was visible even in the planning. Musk, who had believed in the original nonprofit vision, had already left—and he would eventually become the organization's most vocal critic. The researchers who had joined for the purity of the mission would soon face a choice: accept the commercial turn or depart.

The OpenAI that emerged in 2019 would be legally a charity but operationally a tech giant. It would raise billions from Microsoft while claiming the moral authority of a nonprofit. It would build the products that Google had not managed to ship, capturing the market that DeepMind had failed to address.

But the eleven co-founders who had gathered to prevent the concentration of power would begin to scatter. Within four years, only three would remain.

\---

The first cracks appeared in February 2019. OpenAI announced GPT-2 that month—a model with 1.5 billion parameters, ten times larger than anything the lab had previously released. It could generate coherent paragraphs of text, complete stories, answer questions, even write passable code. The researchers who had built it understood they had crossed a threshold. For the first time, a language model was producing output that could be mistaken for human writing.

OpenAI made an unusual decision: they would not release the full model. The stated reason was safety. A system this capable, they argued, could be used to generate misinformation at scale, to automate phishing attacks, to flood the internet with synthetic text indistinguishable from the real thing. They would release only a smaller version, holding back the weights that made the full model work.

The decision was controversial. Critics accused OpenAI of abandoning its founding commitment to openness—the "Open" in the name had always implied transparency, shared research, the opposite of Google's proprietary approach. Supporters argued the lab was doing exactly what it had promised: prioritizing safety over speed, even when speed would have generated publicity.

But the announcement accomplished something else. It established OpenAI as the organization building systems powerful enough to require restriction. The message was not just "we have something dangerous" but "we are the responsible stewards of something dangerous." The withholding was itself a form of marketing.

\---

The following month, OpenAI announced its transformation.

The nonprofit would create a subsidiary: OpenAI LP, structured as what Altman called a "capped-profit" entity. Investors could put money in and earn returns—but those returns would be capped at one hundred times their original investment. Anything above that cap would flow to the nonprofit, to be used for the mission.

The structure was unprecedented. It allowed OpenAI to raise venture capital while claiming the moral authority of a charity. It created equity-like incentives without creating actual equity that could be traded or accumulated indefinitely. It threaded a needle that had never been threaded before: attracting billions of dollars from investors who wanted returns while preserving, at least on paper, the primacy of the mission.

Microsoft invested one billion dollars. The partnership gave OpenAI access to Azure cloud computing—the raw computational power that training large models required—and gave Microsoft a stake in whatever OpenAI built. Satya Nadella had seen what Google was doing with DeepMind; he was not going to let the next generation of AI happen without Microsoft at the table.

The same month, Sam Altman became full-time CEO. He had been co-chairman since the founding, a part-time role alongside his Y Combinator duties. Now he was leaving YC—or being pushed out, depending on whose account you believed—to run OpenAI directly. The transition was framed as a commitment to the mission. The man who had built YC into the most powerful accelerator in tech was stepping away to focus on the most important technology in history.

He would take no equity in the for-profit subsidiary. The decision made some investors nervous; why would a CEO have no financial stake in the company he was building? But the choice preserved Altman's positioning. He could claim to be a neutral steward, motivated by mission rather than money.

\---

The billion-dollar Microsoft deal closed in July 2019, and with it came access to something OpenAI had never had: functionally unlimited compute.

Azure, Microsoft's cloud platform, operated data centers across the world, filled with servers whose processing power could be rented by the hour. For most customers, the expense was prohibitive—training a large neural network could cost hundreds of thousands of dollars in compute time alone. But OpenAI was not a typical customer. Microsoft had invested not just for the returns but for the strategic relationship, and that relationship included favorable terms on the infrastructure OpenAI would need to push the boundaries of what neural networks could do.

The timing was important. Google researchers had published the transformer paper that would reshape the field. OpenAI's researchers understood that the architecture scaled—that if you made models larger and fed them more data, they got better in ways that seemed to have no ceiling. The question was how large, and the answer required more compute than any academic lab could afford.

GPT-2 had been 1.5 billion parameters. The next version would be more than a hundred times larger.

The work began almost immediately. Dario Amodei, the VP of Research, led the technical effort. Tom Brown, a researcher who had joined from Google, drove the architecture decisions. The compute flowed from Microsoft's data centers. The model grew: ten billion parameters, then fifty billion, then a hundred billion, then past a hundred fifty billion. Each increase required more data, more training time, more careful optimization to keep the system from collapsing into incoherence. They were operating at a scale no one had attempted before.

By the spring of 2020, they had something that worked.

GPT-3 could write essays, answer questions, generate code, translate languages, complete stories in the style of authors it had never been trained to imitate. The output was often wrong—the model hallucinated facts, contradicted itself, lost the thread of complex reasoning—but when it worked, it worked in ways that felt qualitatively different from anything that had come before. A user could type a prompt and receive a response that seemed, for a moment, to come from something that understood.

The announcement came in June 2020. OpenAI published a paper describing the model and its capabilities, along with an API that would let developers access the system for a fee. The API was the first significant revenue-generating product OpenAI had ever released. And like with GPT-2, the full model would not be released to the public. OpenAI would control access, deciding who could use the system and for what purposes.

The justification was safety again, but the decision also had commercial implications. If OpenAI released the model, anyone could run it. If they controlled access, they controlled the market.

\---

Inside the organization, the tension that had been forming since the Microsoft deal began to surface. The researchers who had joined for the purity of the mission—to ensure AGI benefited humanity, full stop—were now working for a company that sold API access to the highest bidder. The PPUs that gave them a stake in OpenAI's commercial success aligned their financial interests with growth. But their scientific interests, and in some cases their ethical commitments, pointed elsewhere.

To retain researchers who could command millions at Google or Facebook, OpenAI had implemented "Profit Participation Units." PPUs gave employees a claim on future profits, up to the cap, without granting ownership of the underlying company. The mechanism was clever: it made employees stakeholders in OpenAI's commercial success without diluting the nonprofit's control.

But the PPUs also changed what success meant. A researcher motivated by pure inquiry now had a financial interest in shipping products that generated revenue. The culture began to shift. The lab that had been founded to ensure AGI benefited humanity was becoming, operationally, a company that needed to make money to pay its cap.

Dario Amodei was among those who felt the pull most acutely. He had been at OpenAI since 2016, had helped build GPT-2 and now GPT-3, and had watched the organization evolve from a research lab into something that looked increasingly like a startup with every passing month. The Microsoft deal had not been the end of the transformation but the beginning. More commercial products were in development. More partnerships were being negotiated. The pace of release was accelerating, driven by competitive pressure and the need to generate revenue to justify the investment.

Amodei's concerns were specific. It was not that he opposed commercialization in principle; he understood that the compute costs made pure philanthropy unsustainable. It was that the commercial incentives were shaping research priorities in ways that worried him. The pressure to ship products meant less time for the careful safety work he believed was necessary. The culture was shifting from "move carefully and understand what you're building" toward "move fast and capture the market."

He began talking to his sister, Daniela, who ran safety and policy. He began talking to other researchers who shared his concerns. The conversations were quiet at first, then less so. By the fall of 2020, a group had coalesced around a shared conclusion: OpenAI was no longer the organization they had joined, and they could not change it from inside.

\---

The departures came in December 2020. Dario left first, then Daniela, then Tom Brown, then Chris Olah, then a handful of others. They took with them some of the deepest technical knowledge in the organization, including the researcher who had led the development of GPT-3 and the researcher who had pioneered the interpretability work that might someday let humans understand what neural networks were actually doing.

Within months, they had incorporated Anthropic, raised $124 million from major Silicon Valley figures and firms who shared their concerns, and begun building what they called a "safety-focused" AI company. The structure was different from the start: a public benefit corporation, not a nonprofit with a capped-profit subsidiary. The mission was baked into the corporate form rather than layered on top of it.

"We wanted to build something from scratch," Daniela Amodei said later, "where safety and making humans the center of generative AI was the foundation." Not an add-on. Not a compliance function. The foundation.

Dario, when asked about the departure, offered only that "it is incredibly unproductive to try and argue with someone else's vision." Better, he said, to "take some people you trust and go make your vision happen." The statement was diplomatic. What it elided was the judgment: Altman's vision was not one he could work within. So he stopped working within it.

\---

Back at OpenAI, the departures created a vacuum that was quickly filled. New researchers joined, drawn by the resources and the profile. The commercial products expanded: Codex, a model trained to generate code from natural language; DALL-E, a model that could generate images from text descriptions. Each release generated press coverage, attracted customers, and justified the next round of investment. The flywheel Altman had built was spinning.

Ilya Sutskever stayed. The chief scientist had been there since the beginning, had been recruited by Musk himself, had led the technical work that made GPT-3 possible. His presence gave OpenAI continuity, a link to the founding vision even as the organization transformed around him. But the departure of the Amodeis and their colleagues had changed something. The people who had been most vocal about safety, most skeptical of the commercial turn, were gone. Those who remained were, by selection, more comfortable with the direction.

The months that followed brought more compute, more scale, more capability. GPT-4 was already in development—larger still, more capable still, requiring more infrastructure than GPT-3 had required. Microsoft deepened its integration, building OpenAI's models into Office and Azure and Bing. The revenue grew. The valuation climbed. And in November 2022, almost two years after the Anthropic departures, OpenAI released something that would make everything that came before look like a prologue.

ChatGPT launched as what the company called a "low-key research preview." Within two months, a hundred million people had tried it—though "tried" was the operative word. The product was a parlor trick and a power tool at once: fluent, confident, and frequently wrong. It hallucinated citations, invented facts, and wrote code that almost worked. But people kept coming back. Whatever else could be said about OpenAI's trajectory, the lab had built something that made people want to see what it could do next.

Whether one viral success could sustain the weight of a hundred-billion-dollar valuation and the promise of artificial general intelligence—whether the structure Altman had built could survive contact with a product that actually had to keep working, keep improving, keep justifying the investment—remained to be seen. The answer would arrive, violently, on a Friday afternoon in November 2023.

\---

The board had been investigating for months. This was not, as the narrative would later suggest, a rash decision by academics who did not understand the stakes. By the time Helen Toner, Tasha McCauley, Adam D'Angelo, and Ilya Sutskever voted to remove Sam Altman on November 17, 2023, they had reviewed documentation, interviewed executives, and consulted legal counsel. They understood what they were doing. They believed they had no choice.

The investigation had begun in the spring, triggered by a pattern of discoveries that raised questions about what else the board did not know. ChatGPT had launched in November 2022 without their knowledge—"We found out about it on Twitter," Toner said later—and the product that made OpenAI a household name had been deployed without oversight from the body nominally responsible for ensuring the company's direction served humanity.

Then came the OpenAI Startup Fund—a separate investment vehicle Altman personally owned, whose existence he had failed to disclose. When the board discovered this, Toner said, it "raised questions about what other things might not be true." The fund invested in AI companies whose success was tied directly to OpenAI's. Altman had told the board he held no equity in OpenAI—technically true, and useful for his positioning as a mission-driven steward. What he had not mentioned was the fund.

Then the executives came forward. Two senior leaders, separately, in the months before the firing.

They described, according to Toner, an atmosphere they could not work in. They used the phrase "psychological abuse." They said Altman was not the right person to lead the company to AGI. These were not disgruntled employees; they were executives with direct knowledge of how the organization operated, and they were using language that suggested something beyond ordinary workplace dysfunction.

The board began documenting. They collected what one member later called "screenshots and documentation" of a pattern that extended back years—not just at OpenAI, but before. The pattern was specific: misrepresentations to the board, failures of candor, a gap between what Altman said and what Altman did that had widened until it could no longer be ignored.

There was also the question of board access. During the pandemic, Altman had told Tasha McCauley that he needed to be informed if the board spoke directly to employees—a request some board members viewed as an attempt to limit their oversight.

In early fall 2023, a specific incident crystallized Sutskever's concerns. Altman had elevated another AI researcher, Jakub Pachocki, to director of research—a move Sutskever told his board colleagues reflected a long-running pattern of pitting executives against one another. After Sutskever's departure the following May, Pachocki would become OpenAI's chief technology officer.

Ilya Sutskever compiled his findings in a fifty-two-page memo. The chief scientist—who had been with Altman since the Rosewood dinner in 2015—accused the CEO of "a consistent pattern of lying" and "pitting his executives against each other." Sutskever sent the memo using Gmail's self-destructing message function. It was the kind of precaution you take when you expect your communications to be searched.

\---

The board's lawyers advised caution. Removing a CEO required careful choreography, they said, and publicly stating the reasons could expose them to defamation claims. Whatever evidence they had, they would need to keep it private. The very documentation that justified the firing would be the documentation they could not reveal.

The silence was a strategic choice. It became a strategic catastrophe.

When the board announced the firing on November 17, their three-sentence statement said only that Altman had not been "consistently candid." Within hours, his allies had filled the vacuum. Anonymous sources told reporters the board had acted without warning, without cause, possibly out of personal grievance. The framing was immediate: a visionary leader, a reckless board, a power grab by people who didn't understand the stakes. The board stayed silent. The silence became the story.

The counter-mobilization began within hours, and its mechanics were financial.

Satya Nadella moved on Sunday. The Microsoft CEO announced that the company would hire Altman to lead a new AI research division, and would welcome any OpenAI employees who wanted to follow. If Altman left and the staff followed, OpenAI would be a nonprofit board governing an empty office park. Microsoft would have the talent without the acquisition. The board's authority—their ability to say no—depended on there being something left to govern.

The employee letter arrived Monday—more than seven hundred signatures, threatening mass resignation unless Altman was reinstated. The letter invoked the mission, but the calculation beneath it was simpler. Every OpenAI employee held Profit Participation Units, the compensation structure Altman had implemented in 2019. PPUs gave employees a claim on future profits tied to the company's valuation—a valuation that depended not on the technology, not on the models, not on the mission, but on Altman specifically. The market had priced his continued presence into the enterprise value. Remove him, and the value of every researcher's compensation would crater.

A researcher considering whether to sign faced a question: Was the value of my equity worth more than my concerns about how this company is run? For most employees, the answer was obvious. Years of accumulated wealth hung in the balance. Visionary loyalty, it turned out, was often a high-tech version of a golden handcuff.

Ilya Sutskever signed the letter. Seventy-two hours after voting to fire Altman, the chief scientist was publicly demanding his return. The next day, he posted on X: "I deeply regret my participation in the board's actions. I never intended to harm OpenAI."

The board had authority. Altman had their retirement funds.

On Wednesday, Altman walked back in. Toner and McCauley were removed from the board.

In May 2024, six months after his reinstatement, Sam Altman's position had never been stronger.

The board that had tried to remove him was gone. Bret Taylor had taken the chair; Larry Summers joined; eventually Altman himself took a seat—the man they had fired now sitting on the body that was supposed to oversee him. A law firm, WilmerHale, had investigated the board's process and found the firing unjustified; the investigation's scope was limited, its full findings never released. The new directors understood what the previous board had not: that their authority existed only so long as it was never exercised.

The employees who had signed the letter threatening resignation remained at their desks, their PPUs intact, their equity climbing with the company's valuation. Microsoft had deepened its commitment, and OpenAI was negotiating new funding rounds that would value the company at more than $150 billion.

Then the departures began.

\---

Ilya Sutskever announced he was leaving on May 14. His departure statement praised OpenAI's "miraculous" trajectory and predicted the company would "build AGI that is both safe and beneficial." He said nothing about the memo, nothing about his vote, nothing about what had compelled his public reversal. His next venture would speak for him: within months, he incorporated Safe Superintelligence Inc. SSI would focus exclusively on safety-first AI development. No consumer products. No revenue pressure.

The same week, Jan Leike resigned. Leike had led OpenAI's Superalignment team, the group tasked with ensuring that artificial general intelligence—if it ever arrived—would remain under human control. He had joined believing that OpenAI was the best place to do that work. He left believing the opposite.

"Safety culture and processes have taken a backseat to shiny products," Leike wrote in his resignation statement. His team had been "sailing against the wind," struggling for compute while the commercial teams consumed the company's resources. The diagnosis was specific and damning: the organization that claimed to be building AGI safely was systematically deprioritizing the work required to make that claim true.

Leike's destination was Anthropic. The same month, Vox published an investigation into OpenAI's departure agreements.

Employees who left the company faced a choice: sign documents that included non-disparagement clauses and retain their equity, or refuse and forfeit their stake. The Profit Participation Units that had been designed to retain talent were now being used as leverage to silence criticism. Researchers who had spent years building the systems OpenAI was deploying could not discuss their concerns publicly without losing their financial stake in the work.

One researcher, Daniel Kokotajlo, refused. Kokotajlo had grown concerned about the pace of OpenAI's development and the adequacy of its safety measures. When presented with the departure agreement, he declined to sign. He understood what it meant: the equity he had earned would be clawed back. He chose to speak anyway.

His decision triggered a Senate investigation. Legislators demanded to know whether other AI companies were using similar mechanisms to suppress internal criticism. The practice was legal, unremarkable in standard corporate terms. The product was artificial intelligence. The silenced critics were safety researchers.

Altman's response was characteristic. "I did not know this was happening," he said, "and I should have."

In August 2024, John Schulman announced he was leaving. Schulman was a co-founder. He had been at OpenAI since the beginning, had architected ChatGPT, had built the reinforcement learning systems that made the product possible. Of all the researchers who had built what OpenAI had become, Schulman had the deepest technical claim to the work. If anyone had earned the right to stay and shape the company's direction, it was him.

He was going to Anthropic.

"I've decided to pursue this goal"—AI alignment—"at Anthropic, where I believe I can deepen my focus on AI alignment," he wrote. The statement was measured, almost bland. It did not criticize Altman or OpenAI by name. It simply noted that the work he cared about most might be better pursued elsewhere.

By the end of 2024, the arithmetic was stark. Of the eleven people who had gathered in 2015 to announce a billion-dollar nonprofit dedicated to ensuring artificial general intelligence benefited humanity, three remained at OpenAI. Musk was gone, locked in litigation. Sutskever was gone, his concerns documented in a memo no one could read. The Amodeis were running the competition. Schulman and Leike had joined them. The researchers who understood the systems best, who had built the products that made OpenAI famous, who had the technical credibility to assess whether the work was being done safely—they had made their judgment with their feet.

Sam Altman remained.

\---

On January 21, 2025, Sam Altman stood in the Roosevelt Room of the White House beside the President of the United States.

Donald Trump had been inaugurated the day before. Now he sat behind a small desk positioned for the cameras, flanked by Masayoshi Son of SoftBank, Larry Ellison of Oracle, and Altman. Trump announced what he called "the largest AI infrastructure project in history." Stargate. Five hundred billion dollars committed over four years. Data centers across the United States. One hundred thousand jobs. The first facility—one million square feet of computing infrastructure—was already under construction in Abilene, Texas.

Son spoke of his commitment to America. Ellison spoke of energy infrastructure. When Altman's turn came, he was careful: "This will be the most important project of this era for our country and maybe the most important project."

The hedge was characteristic. Maybe the most important. Altman had spent a decade learning which claims to make directly and which to qualify.

Elon Musk was not in the room. He had co-founded OpenAI with Altman, had provided crucial early funding, had helped recruit Ilya Sutskever from Google. But he had quit in February 2018 when his plan to take control was rejected. Now he watched from the outside, a co-founder excluded from the moment his former partner was making history.

Within hours, Musk posted his assessment. "They don't actually have the money," he wrote. "SoftBank has well under $10B secured. I have that on good authority."

Altman's reply was immediate: "wrong, as you surely know."

The truth was somewhere in the margin between a commitment and a check. What mattered was not whether Musk was right about the current state of funding—it was why an announcement of this scale had become necessary.

\---

The financial position of OpenAI in January 2025 was unlike anything the industry had seen—ten billion dollars in annualized revenue, five billion in losses, commitments dwarfing both.

The revenue number would have seemed fantastical three years earlier, when the company was a research lab with a nonprofit charter and no consumer product. ChatGPT had created a market that had not existed before November 2022. Altman had captured it, riding the product's viral adoption to a valuation that climbed past five hundred billion dollars.

But the costs of staying in the race were climbing faster than the revenue.

In 2024, OpenAI lost five billion dollars. The company was projecting one hundred forty-three billion in cumulative negative cash flow through 2029. It had committed to more than 1.4 trillion dollars in chips, data centers, and computing power over the next eight years—contractual obligations, negotiated with Nvidia and Microsoft and datacenter operators who required binding commitments before allocating scarce resources.

OpenAI was not a company that had built a profitable business and was now expanding. It was a company whose spending was outpacing its revenue, whose commitments were outpacing its spending, and whose survival depended on continued confidence from partners who had already invested too much to walk away.

Altman understood this dynamic intimately. His career had been a study in how to construct webs of mutual obligation. At Loopt, the investors who might have replaced him after the management complaints had their own reputations tied to his success. At Y Combinator, the partners who might have forced a cleaner break had built the institution's brand around his leadership. At OpenAI in November 2023, the employees who might have accepted the board's judgment had equity that was worthless without him.

Now the same logic was operating at civilizational scale. Microsoft had committed thirteen billion dollars and integrated OpenAI's models into products used by millions of people. Nvidia was negotiating a hundred billion more. SoftBank, Oracle, MGX, the sovereign wealth funds circling the company—each new partner became another stakeholder in OpenAI's survival.

The Stargate announcement made sense only in this light. It was not a declaration of strength but a maneuver to bind more capital before it could flow elsewhere—to ensure that the institutions most capable of funding alternatives would instead be committed to OpenAI's success.

\---

Throughout the past years, Apple and OpenAI were discussing terms that could have changed everything. Apple was rebuilding Siri. The voice assistant had been the first of its kind when it launched in 2011, but fourteen years later it had become an industry joke—unable to match the capabilities of ChatGPT, embarrassing to use in comparison with the new generation of AI assistants. Apple needed a modern language model to power the rebuild.

The terms, according to the Financial Times, would have meant billions in guaranteed revenue. Distribution to every iPhone user on earth—more than a billion active devices. A partnership with the most valuable company in the world.

OpenAI made "a conscious decision" to decline.

Altman had been building toward a different vision for months—a vision that required turning down Apple's billions because accepting them would have meant abandoning something larger.

Five months earlier, in May 2025, OpenAI had announced the acquisition of Jony Ive's design studio for 6.4 billion dollars. Ive was the designer who had shaped Apple's most important products over three decades—the iMac that saved the company in 1998, the iPod that created the digital music industry, the iPhone that redefined personal computing. He had left Apple in 2019 and founded a small studio that took on select projects. Now he was bringing that studio to OpenAI, where he would lead design across the entire company.

The acquisition signaled Altman's intentions. OpenAI was not going to remain a software company licensing models to hardware makers. It was going to build its own hardware—a device that Altman described as a "third core device," something that would sit alongside phones and laptops rather than replacing them. Screenless. Pocketable. A new category of object that would bring "peace and calm" rather than the "unsettling" notifications of current technology. OpenAI expected to ship one hundred million units "faster than any company has ever shipped 100 million of something new before"—outpacing the iPhone itself. A software company with no hardware experience, no manufacturing relationships, no retail distribution, competing against the company that had spent two decades building advantages in exactly those domains.

By declining the Siri deal, Altman chose to compete with Apple rather than power it. The billions in guaranteed revenue that could have subsidized OpenAI's burn rate went instead to Google. The distribution to a billion devices that could have protected OpenAI's market position was now strengthening its closest technical competitor. OpenAI would have to succeed at something entirely new while simultaneously maintaining its position in the AI capabilities race against competitors who were focused entirely on models.

\---

The strategic picture that emerged in late 2025 illuminated just how exposed that position was.

OpenAI was competing in consumer chatbots against Gemini Google's Gemini, in enterprise APIs against Anthropic's Claude, in image and video generation against a proliferating field of specialists—while simultaneously pursuing hardware, voice assistants, healthcare, and robotics investments. Each front required capital, engineering talent, and management attention.

Against this, two competitors had made different choices.

Anthropic had focused with unusual discipline on a single market. Claude was not trying to be a consumer sensation—the company had no viral chatbot moment, no Super Bowl advertisement, no celebrity partnerships. It was not building hardware or generating images or producing videos. Anthropic had one product aimed at one market: enterprise customers who needed AI capabilities integrated into their businesses. The company that Dario and Daniela Amodei had built after leaving OpenAI was structured around a thesis—that the way to win was to do one thing extraordinarily well rather than many things adequately.

The enterprise customers were proving them right. A chart compiled by Menlo Ventures from portfolio company data tracked which AI models enterprise customers were actually paying for. In 2023, when ChatGPT's dominance seemed unassailable, OpenAI had commanded fifty percent of the enterprise market. Anthropic held twelve percent. By 2025, the positions had nearly inverted. OpenAI had fallen to twenty-seven percent. Anthropic had risen to forty.

Meanwhile, Google had the opposite advantage. It was not focused, but it did not need to be—because it already had everything OpenAI was trying to build.

Distribution came through Android, Chrome, and Search: three billion users who encountered Google products daily, channels through which Gemini could reach customers without a dollar of marketing spend. Cash flow came from the most profitable advertising business in history, generating margins that could absorb any level of AI investment indefinitely. Infrastructure had been built over two decades—not just cloud services but custom silicon: the Tensor Processing Units that Google had been designing since 2015, purpose-built for machine learning workloads, running in data centers no competitor could replicate. Training data came from the largest corpus ever assembled: the index behind Search, the emails in Gmail, the documents in Drive, the videos on YouTube. When Apple chose a partner for the Siri rebuild, Google had everything Apple needed. OpenAI had the models. Google had everything else.

OpenAI was trying to build from scratch what Google already possessed, while simultaneously trying to match the focus that was allowing Anthropic to win enterprise customers. Success required winning on multiple fronts at once against competitors who had each chosen to be strong where OpenAI was stretched thin.

\---

The consumer market was showing the strain. A Financial Times analysis tracked ChatGPT's mobile app downloads through 2025. In the spring, downloads had surged past one hundred million per month—the kind of viral growth that justified valuations and invited comparisons to the iPhone launch. Through the summer, growth plateaued.

By October 2025, ChatGPT was at eighty-five million monthly downloads. Gemini, Google's offering, had surged from twenty million to nearly eighty million over the same period. The gap that had seemed insurmountable eighteen months earlier—the moat that ChatGPT's first-mover advantage was supposed to have created—was closing.

As the market share eroded, the responses came faster. On January 16, 2026, OpenAI announced it would begin testing advertisements in ChatGPT.

The ads would appear at the bottom of responses for free users and subscribers to the lower-cost tier. Plus, Pro, and Enterprise customers would remain ad-free. Sarah Friar, OpenAI's chief financial officer, framed the decision as expanding access: "so more people can benefit from our tools with fewer usage limits."

An industry analyst offered a different interpretation: "Ads aren't a distraction from the gen AI race. They're how OpenAI stays in it."

Altman himself had once described advertising as a "last resort"—the revenue model you accepted when your product couldn't sustain itself through direct payment. The framing had positioned OpenAI as different from the advertising-dependent giants of the previous technology generation. Now that framing was being abandoned. The man who claimed he didn't need equity because he had "enough" was presiding over a company that needed bottom-of-the-screen ad revenue to pay for Texas data centers. The company that had raised more venture capital than almost any startup in history, that had announced a five-hundred-billion-dollar infrastructure project two weeks earlier, was introducing ads to a product that was supposed to be so valuable it didn't need them. The mission had met the market.

In the same weeks, Altman was in the Middle East. Bloomberg reported in January 2026 that he was seeking fifty billion dollars from sovereign wealth funds in Abu Dhabi and Saudi Arabia. The round would value OpenAI at somewhere between seven hundred fifty billion and eight hundred thirty billion dollars—a valuation that exceeded all but a handful of public companies, for a company that had lost five billion dollars the previous year.

The negotiations were preliminary. Term sheets had not been signed. But the direction was unmistakable. OpenAI needed more capital than American venture markets were providing, or would provide at the terms Altman required. The company that had been founded to ensure artificial intelligence benefited humanity was now seeking investment from the sovereign wealth fund controlled by Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, whose government ordered the murder of Washington Post columnist Jamal Khashoggi and dismembered his body in a consulate

\---

The corporate structure itself had transformed. In October 2025, OpenAI completed its conversion from a capped-profit subsidiary of a nonprofit into a public benefit corporation. The nonprofit that had been created in 2015 to ensure the mission would survive commercial pressure still existed, technically. It would receive equity in the new company. Its effective authority had been demonstrated two years earlier, when it tried to exercise its oversight function and was dismantled within five days.

Microsoft emerged from the restructuring with a twenty-seven percent stake—worth roughly one hundred thirty-five billion dollars at the new valuation. The company that had provided thirteen billion dollars in funding, that had integrated OpenAI's models into Office and Azure and Bing, that had defended Altman when the board tried to remove him, was now a permanent partner with a permanent claim on OpenAI's future.

Reports had surfaced that the board was discussing equity for Altman himself. For nearly a decade, Altman had built moral authority partly on his lack of ownership.

Now the board was discussing terms. Altman told employees there were "no plans" for a "giant equity stake." Bret Taylor, the board chairman, confirmed that discussions had occurred but "no specific figures" had been decided. The careful phrasing left room for what everyone understood was coming.

Musk's lawsuit proceeded toward trial. The case alleged that OpenAI's conversion to a for-profit structure violated the founding agreements and the representations Musk had relied on when providing early funding. The judge had found "plenty of evidence" that OpenAI's leaders had made assurances about maintaining the nonprofit structure—assurances that, if a jury believed them, could complicate or block the conversion. OpenAI had countersued, accusing Musk of orchestrating a "sham bid" to acquire the company for ninety-seven billion dollars. The trial was scheduled for March 2026.

The co-founders who had stood together at the Rosewood Hotel dinner in 2015, united in their fear that Google would build artificial general intelligence without sufficient concern for safety, were now locked in litigation over whether the company they had created together had betrayed its founding purpose.

\---

Sam Altman remains.

He had stood in the Roosevelt Room announcing the largest infrastructure project in history, sought fifty billion dollars from sovereign wealth funds in the Middle East, tested advertisements in the product that had made him famous. He was building a device to compete with the iPhone and converting his nonprofit into a company he could own. The pattern of his career held: as pressure mounted, the deals grew larger. At Loopt, when the product failed, he negotiated an exit that let everyone call failure success. At Y Combinator, when partners confronted him, he maneuvered into a title the partnership never approved. At OpenAI in November 2023, when the board tried to remove him, he returned within five days and they were gone instead.

Is this time different?

The competitors are not failing products or procedural opponents or board members who can be outmaneuvered. They are two forces closing from different directions.

From one side: Anthropic. The company the Amodeis built after leaving, structured around a single conviction: that the way to win was to do one thing extraordinarily well.

From the other side: Google. The entrenched giant with three billion users, the most profitable advertising business in history, two decades of infrastructure, and the patience to wait. Google had lost the first round to ChatGPT's viral launch. Now Gemini was closing the consumer gap. Google had won the Apple contract OpenAI turned down. Google could fund this race indefinitely without strain.

The gap is closing from multiple directions. The burn rate is accelerating. The dependencies are multiplying. And for the first time in Altman's career, the question is not whether he can assemble the next deal, bind the next partner, or weave the next strand of obligation. The question is whether the web will hold.

Paul Graham's assessment, offered when Altman was twenty-eight, has always been precise. You could parachute him into an island full of cannibals, Graham said, and come back in five years, and he'd be the king.

Graham did not describe what would happen next.
